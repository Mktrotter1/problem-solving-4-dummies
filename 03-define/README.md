# Step 3: Define

> *What exactly is the problem?*

You've observed. You have raw data. Now compress it into a clear statement of what's actually wrong.

## What This Step Is

Defining the problem is the single most important step in the entire sequence. A well-defined problem is half-solved. A poorly-defined one will waste all the effort that follows.

This is where you turn a pile of observations into a single, precise statement: **"The problem is ___."**

## How to Do It

**Write a problem statement.** One or two sentences. If you can't write it down clearly, you don't understand it yet.

A good problem statement has:
- **What** is wrong (not why — that's step 4)
- **Where** it occurs (scope/boundary)
- **When** it occurs (conditions/triggers)
- **How big** it is (impact/severity)

**Separate the problem from the symptom.** "Revenue is down" is a symptom. "Customers are abandoning checkout when shipping costs appear" is closer to a problem. The distinction matters because they lead to completely different solutions.

**State what you know and what you don't know.** Make two lists. The "don't know" list is just as valuable as the "know" list — it tells you where to dig next.

**Draw a boundary.** What's inside the problem? What's outside? What are you *not* trying to solve?

## Techniques That Help Here

### The Feynman Technique
Try to explain the problem in plain language, as if to someone with no context. Where your explanation gets vague or hand-wavy — that's where your understanding is weak. Those gaps *are* the problem you haven't fully defined yet.

### Rubber Duck Debugging
Same principle: explain the problem out loud, step by step. The act of articulation forces precision. You'll often hear yourself say something that reveals the real issue.

### "How Might We..." (from Design Thinking)
Reframe the problem as a question: "How might we [desired outcome] without [constraint]?" This forces you to be specific about both the goal and the boundaries.

## Common Mistakes

- **Defining the solution instead of the problem.** "We need a new database" is a solution. "Query response times exceed acceptable thresholds under current load" is a problem. Define the problem first; solutions come in step 6.
- **Being too vague.** "Communication is bad" means nothing. "The night shift doesn't receive updates about changes made during the day shift" is something you can solve.
- **Being too broad.** "Fix everything" isn't a problem statement. Narrow it until you can act on it.
- **Skipping this step entirely.** This is the #1 cause of wasted effort in problem-solving. If you do nothing else from this cookbook, do this step.

## The Key Question

**"Can I write down, in one or two sentences, what exactly is wrong?"**

If yes, and it's specific enough to act on, move to [Step 4: Understand](../04-understand/).
If not, go back to [Step 2: Observe](../02-observe/).
