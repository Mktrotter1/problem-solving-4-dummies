# Step 10: Reflect

> *What did I learn?*

The problem is solved. Almost everyone stops here. Don't.

## What This Step Is

Reflection means examining the entire process you just went through — not just the solution, but how you got to it. What worked? What didn't? What would you do differently?

This is Polya's step 4. It's the step that separates people who solve the same kinds of problems over and over from people who get better at solving problems in general.

## How to Do It

**Review the path.** Look at steps 1-9. Where did you spend the most time? Where did you get stuck? Where did you backtrack? Those friction points are where your problem-solving process is weakest — and therefore where it can improve the most.

**Ask: "Was there a faster path?"** Not to beat yourself up, but to notice patterns. Could you have gotten to the root cause faster? Was there a solution option you dismissed too early?

**Ask: "What would I do differently next time?"** Be specific. "I'd spend more time on step 3" isn't useful. "I'd write down the problem statement before opening the code" is.

**Ask: "What surprised me?"** Surprises are where learning lives. Anything that contradicted your expectations is a signal about a gap in your mental model.

**Ask: "Can I generalize this?"** Is there a principle or pattern here that applies beyond this specific problem? If so, name it and remember it. This is how you build problem-solving intuition.

**Document it if it matters.** If others might face this problem, leave a trail. Not a novel — just enough that the next person can skip the dead ends you explored.

## Techniques That Help Here

### Bloom's Taxonomy (Self-Assessment)

Check which cognitive level you operated at:

| Level | Question |
|-------|----------|
| **Remember** | Can I recall the key facts of this problem? |
| **Understand** | Can I explain why the solution works? |
| **Apply** | Could I apply this approach to a similar problem? |
| **Analyze** | Can I break down *why* this approach worked and others didn't? |
| **Evaluate** | Can I judge the quality of my solution and process? |
| **Create** | Can I synthesize a general principle from this experience? |

The higher you can go, the more transferable your learning.

### After-Action Review (AAR)

Four questions, in order:

1. What was supposed to happen?
2. What actually happened?
3. Why was there a difference?
4. What will we do differently next time?

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping this step entirely.** This is the most-skipped step. It's also the step that makes every future problem easier.
- **Being too vague.** "I learned a lot" isn't reflection. What specifically did you learn?
- **Only reflecting on failures.** Successes are worth examining too. *Why* did it work? Was it your process, your intuition, or luck? Knowing the difference matters.
- **Reflecting alone.** If others were involved, reflect together. They saw things you didn't.

## The Key Question

**"What do I know now that I didn't know at step 1, and how does it change how I'll approach the next problem?"**
