# Step 6: Generate Options

> *What could I do about it?*

Now — *only now* — do you start thinking about solutions. And the critical discipline here is to think of *more than one*.

## What This Step Is

Generating options means coming up with multiple possible paths from where you are (the problem) to where you want to be (done). Not committing to any of them yet. Just listing what's possible.

The goal is divergence — spread wide before you narrow down. The first idea that pops into your head is rarely the best one. It's just the most available.

## How to Do It

**Generate at least three options.** One option is a trap. Two options is a dilemma. Three or more is a choice. Force yourself past the first idea.

**Separate generation from evaluation.** Don't judge ideas while you're creating them. That kills creativity. Generate first, evaluate in step 7.

**Vary the approaches.** If all your options are variations on the same idea, you haven't really generated options. Push for fundamentally different approaches:

- What's the quick fix?
- What's the thorough fix?
- What's the approach that eliminates the problem entirely?
- What if we did nothing?
- What would someone outside this domain try?

**Ask: "How has this been solved before?"** Almost no problem is truly novel. Look for prior art. What did other people/teams/industries do when they faced something similar?

## Techniques That Help Here

### Inversion

"What would make this problem *worse*?" List those things. Now invert each one into a potential solution. This reliably produces options your forward-thinking brain misses.

### Six Thinking Hats (de Bono)

Force yourself through six perspectives on each option idea:

| Hat | Ask |
|-----|-----|
| **White** | What are the facts? |
| **Red** | What's my gut reaction? |
| **Black** | What could go wrong? |
| **Yellow** | What's the best case? |
| **Green** | What's a creative alternative? |
| **Blue** | Am I thinking about this the right way? |

### TRIZ (for Inventive Problems)

If you're facing a contradiction ("I need X but X prevents Y"), TRIZ offers 40 inventive principles derived from analyzing 200,000+ patents. The core insight: most "novel" problems have been solved before in a different field. The solution often involves resolving the contradiction rather than compromising.

### Analogical Reasoning

"What does this remind me of?" Map solutions from other domains. The problem of distributing load across servers was solved by looking at how telephone exchanges route calls. Look sideways.

### Lateral Thinking (de Bono)

Deliberately approach from an unexpected angle. Techniques:

- **Random entry:** pick a random word and force connections to your problem
- **Provocation:** make a deliberately absurd statement and work backward to something useful
- **Challenge:** question *why* things are done the current way — not to criticize, but to find hidden alternatives

## Common Mistakes

- **Falling in love with the first idea.** Your brain rewards you for "solving" the problem. That reward comes at the moment of the first idea, not the best idea. Don't stop there.
- **Anchoring.** Once you hear one option, every subsequent option gets unconsciously compared to it. Generate independently if possible.
- **Self-censoring.** "That would never work" kills ideas before they're explored. At this stage, nothing is off the table.
- **Generating alone.** Other people's brains work differently than yours. They'll see options you literally cannot see. Ask them.

## The Key Question

**"What are at least three fundamentally different ways I could get from the problem to done?"**

Write them down. Then move to [Step 7: Choose](../07-choose/).
